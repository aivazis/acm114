Objectives

This course is an introduction to concurrent algorithms and programming techniques with a focus on scientific applications. I will try to pay equal attention to the implications of concurrency on algorithms, numerical techniques and software engineering.

I will try to make as few assumptions about your background as possible. I will present an overview of the basic ideas in algorithm design and analysis, discuss relevant data structures as the need arises, and walk through short introductions to all the numerical techniques we will be investigating. However, I am assuming that you are competent in some programming language, with a strong preference for C or C++, although I will try to accommodate FORTRAN programmers. You will need programming skills in order to complete some of the required course work. Also, some of the later material will require a superficial knowledge of python, an interpreted language that is becoming a common fixture in high performance computing.

We will be making extensive use of MPI, the de facto standard environment for scientific computing, and the only widely available solution for writing parallel programs that scale to thousands of processors. We will cover both basic and advanced techniques, including some of the improvements from version 2.0 of the standard. You should expect to be a comfortable, and perhaps sophisticated, MPI user by the end of the term.

Organization

There will be no official homework assignments this quarter. I will assign exercises throughout the quarter, which we be collected and graded but will not count towards your course grade. Instead, your grade will be determined by two presentation you will be asked to give.

The first presentation is based on your -- perhaps partial -- results from last quarter. I expect a 15 minute presentation on the motivation for your choice of project, the identification of the portion that was expected to benefit from concurrency, your parallelization strategy, and any performance and scaling results you were able to obtain. You should wrap up your presentation with your project plan for this quarter. You may choose to continue improving your project from last quarter, or start a new one.

The second presentation will happen towards the end of the quarter. Its focus will be on your final project results, with a structure very similar to the first presentation.
